*The birth of [[BBEG's Son]]*

As your vision fades, you feel your mind pulled through time and space. Slowly and gently, the darkness gives way to warmth, and you open your eyes.

Sunlight spills across white linen. You face out an open window. Beyond it you hear the soft, muffled, sounds of a fair summer's day: birdsong in the eaves, a cart rolling over distant stones, children laughing somewhere down the road. 

A breeze carries in the smell of freshly-cut grass and summer flowers. With the breeze, your vision drifts inside the room, where everything is still pale and bright.

In the center of the room, a series of bowed heads with bent shoulders and busy hands surround a bedpost decorated with prayer beads. A basin glimmers with clean water. 

A woman on the bed cries out in pain, and the birds outside scatter.

“Breathe, my lady,” says one of the midwives. “By [[Salorna]]'s wisdom, breathe.”

Another voice, older and thinner, murmurs a prayer beneath her breath.

A man beside the bed kneels in a shaft of sunlight raining down from above. His hands are clasped so tightly around a holy pendant that the chain has begun to cut his palms. He whispers the same prayer to himself again and again.

"You've got to push, my lady!" says another midwife.

The woman cries in pain until her screams are joined by another.

And then another.

The midwives fall silent for a single breath. The woman on the bed sobs, too weak now for words. You see the shape of a child quickly wrapped in cloth, held in hands that seemingly do not know whether to cradle it or hide it.

The cloth slips for but a moment and your vision clarifies.

Two mouths. Two chins. Two newborn faces conjoined where a single nose should be. 

Two voices, screaming in unison.

Between their cries, you make out the man's cyclical prayer, more rapid and anxious than before.

One of the midwives gasps, “Mercy.”

The older woman says, “Peace now. Mind your tongue and tend to the child.”

Hands shaking, the midwife moves anxiously towards the praying man, holding the child at a distance.

"My lord", she says quietly, "your son."

The mother reaches from the bed. “My son?” she whispers faintly.

The man looks up from his prayer. His breath catches as if he has seen some unholy scripture written there across his child's faces.

“No,” he says softly, taking a step back.

In gasping breaths the mother makes out, "I have a son", before collapsing, arms reaching a child she'll never see.

The older woman takes charge.
"Quick, get them out of here!"
"Stay with us my lady. Just keep breathing."

Amid the flurry of action, the man is hurried to the door and presented his child once more. 

The father does not take the child before your vision is dragged back through the room, past the midwives, past the body of a dying mother, past a basin now stained red with blood, and out a window; back into darkness.
